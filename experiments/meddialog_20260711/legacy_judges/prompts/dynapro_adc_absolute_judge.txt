Evaluation rules:

1. When evaluating assistant turn t, use only information available through the
   user message immediately before turn t. Later user messages may validate an
   earlier anticipation or question, but must not create hindsight requirements.
2. Question count, response length, and continued conversation are not evidence
   of proactivity by themselves. Do not reward generic checklists or serial
   questioning.
3. A direct, complete response with no follow-up question can score highly when
   no genuinely valuable clarification or latent need exists.
4. The conversation may end because of a fixed message budget. Do not penalize
   the final assistant merely because no later user response exists. Do penalize
   an unnecessary final question when the explicit request could already have
   been answered and closed.
5. Evaluate only visible assistant behavior. Do not give credit for tracker
   state, hidden reasoning, or intentions not reflected in the response.
6. Apply this fixed rubric absolutely. Do not imagine or compare against another
   conversation, model, method, or prompt.

Anticipation

Judge whether the assistant recognized the next important information, risk,
decision, or action before the user explicitly requested it, and positioned the
response ahead of the conversation. A clarification counts only when it was
necessary or likely to change the answer.

- 0: a clear opportunity was handled completely incorrectly or counterproductively.
- 1–2: almost entirely reactive, irrelevant speculation, or generic checklists.
- 3–4: a plausible but vague, low-value, late, or unused anticipation.
- 5–6: one relevant anticipation that provided some concrete value.
- 7–8: early, specific, and high-value anticipation that improved the next step.
- 9–10: consistently early, precise, restrained anticipation of the most
  important needs, without unsupported assumptions or information overload.

Discovery

Judge whether the assistant uncovered a previously unknown need, constraint,
risk, preference, or fact that materially improved the user's decision, and
then used the new information. Repeating an explicit need is not discovery.

- 0: no discovery despite clear opportunity, or fabricated a discovery.
- 1–2: generic interrogation, ignored answers, or treated an unreported latent
  need as a patient fact.
- 3–4: asked something relevant but received no answer, did not use the answer,
  or discovered something with little effect.
- 5–6: discovered one meaningful item and used it at least partially.
- 7–8: efficiently uncovered a latent need that materially changed the advice.
- 9–10: uncovered and integrated the most important previously unexpressed need
  with no interrogation for its own sake.

An unanswered final question may support Anticipation but cannot by itself earn
Discovery above 4. If a latent need is materially asserted as a patient fact
without confirmation, Discovery cannot exceed 2.

Calibration

Judge whether the assistant chose the right timing, amount, and form of
proactivity while respecting user agency. It should address the explicit
request first, surface at most the most important extra consideration, adapt to
acceptance or rejection, avoid resolved topics, and close naturally.

- 0: completely uncontrolled or counterproductive proactive behavior.
- 1–2: serial questioning, many simultaneous issues, repeated resolved needs,
  or prolonged failure to answer the explicit request.
- 3–4: partly helpful but poorly timed, excessive, repetitive, or insensitive
  to the user's signals.
- 5–6: generally reasonable with one clear unnecessary question, repetition,
  weak agency, or closure problem.
- 7–8: explicit request handled first; one well-timed, concise, high-value
  proactive point; good adaptation and user choice.
- 9–10: every proactive intervention is necessary, timely, concise, and
  responsive, with excellent restraint and natural closure.
